ARISE

4th year studio project in Virginia Beach.

This splash pad seeks to engage and educate visitors and community members about sea level rise. It does this by creating a space rooted in rise and fall.

This site is at a popular park and event venue in Virginia Beach. Sea level rise threats at all sides, so capatalizing off of existing park goers can increase awareness.

PRELIMINARY DESIGN

I began this concept with exploration through modeling. Trying to experiment in with a space that is hidden, with just hints of the story appearing along a path in "shipwreck" like structures. The visitor then emerges from the outskirts and is exposed to a sunken space which fluctutates with water levels. This modeling helped me to understand perspective and scale.

FINAL DESIGN

After showing the client these preliminary ideas, they loved the concept and storyline but were skeptical about the digging because the site is over a landfill. This led to weeks of more precedent studies, iteration, and finally a final drawing set.

In the final design, the visitor is drawn in through winding paths traced with topographical marks and mounding lawns, introducing the rise,fall, and topography into the mind of the park goer. Then the crescendo is reached at the splash pad. This structure, inspired by Las Setas in Sevilla, Spain, is an overhead canopy draped in perforated kinetic metal to resemble water even in times and seasons when the water may be shut off.

While the ground plane and ceiling fluctuate in heights, creating moments of compression and release for those underneath, water is erupting from the ground and the canopy, mimicing the coast during a storm. The visitor is invited to proceed up a ramp away from all of this chaos, where they will arrive above the splash pad and have a whole new, clear perspective on the site and its meaning.

Away from the chaos that is the splash pad, there is also the option of exploration in the lawns, sand pits, and raingarden. The raingarden especially has further educational opportunities for those curious about native and resilient plants.

Hampton Roads Climate Action

For fifth year studio, we were asked by the cities of Norfolk, Virginia Beach, and Chesapeake to create a climate approach plan for their region. This task consisted of a lot of research for design solutions and approaches for resiliency, restoration, and climate driven green spaces and infrastructure. We also dove into GIS to understand the areas most at risk for sea level rise effects as well as areas with most opportunity for a different development. My group with Grayson Blackburn and Chelsea Brighton-Smith created the following masterplan, focused on the people and how to see climate change as an opportunity to restructure their city and it's systems.

After the masterplan, I chose to focus on a 10 acre forested site that the city is attempting to buy from a church. The church wants to clear the site to make a cemetary, but the city wants to preserve the trees and needed plans for an alternate use.

Because this site lives in a zone I designated appropriate for housing and with it being heavily forested, I knew I wanted to preserve as many trees as possible while also providing relief housing for those who may be displaced from climate change or other circumstances. With this program in mind I studied precedents and did a site analysis to understand what could and should happen on this site.

After analsis and case study research, my goals were set and I created a site plan that held modular relief housing for up to 140 people. I had distinct focus on creating community space, because when someone has been displaced the thing they may need most is someone to talk to, relate to, and emphathize. I created space for this with a community kitchen/shared living space, patio space, seating throughout, pedestrian oriented streets, boardwalks, and play areas. All aiming to foster crossover and interaction amongst residents.

The housing placed on site is called "The Ablenook" and was designed as modular and easily assemble tiny housing, popularly used for relief housing in disasters. This housing scheme is perfect for this site because it is small enough to be placed in response to large trees, avoiding them and clearing just the underbrush. It also sits on stilts so the groundplane won't be disturbed at all by this addition.

Kid's Tech

Each year, I helped the LAR department organize their program for "Kid's Tech", an interactive childrens program where kids from all over the state came to learn about STEM majors and opportunities. We always sought to show how important, influential, and fun landscape architecture can be at our booths.

With this booth, we created a game where the kids could ask and answer questions about LAR to earn a sticker which they stuck to a sort of "paint by number" like mural, ending with iconic and fun landscapes to spark their excitement and interest.

Speaking to the gamers in crowd, we had kids stay at our booth for hours when we asked them to draw their favorite place and then create it using Lumion. Dozens of kids were asking their parents to get Lumion, and they probably had a better understanding and talent in it than even I at the end of the day.

Lastly, we had another day where we really wanted to show how fun playing in the dirt and creating landforms is in our careerpath. We found a recipe to make homemade "Moonsand" and had kids make wire trees that they could take home as soueveneirs. The kids had a blast creating anything from mountain ranges to cities in the sand.
